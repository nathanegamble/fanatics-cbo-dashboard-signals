# Contextual Notes Playbook for Claude Cowork

This playbook describes the repeatable approach for writing executive-ready Contextual Notes for the Fanatics CBO dashboard.

The goal is to turn dashboard data plus external sports/culture signals into concise, additive commentary for Cameron. The notes should help the reader understand what is moving demand, why it matters commercially, and where fast-turn emphasis may help capture the moment.

This document is a writing and review guide. It does **not** replace the dashboard data, the raw reports, or the public signal feeds. Use it to shape how Contextual Notes are selected, phrased, and fact-checked.

---

## 1. Audience and purpose

The Contextual Notes are written directly for the executive reader.

They should:

- add external, cultural, category, or commercial context;
- connect that context to the data section it appears beside;
- sound like a seasoned sports commerce/category analyst with brand-performance fluency;
- be objective, additive, and actionable without coaching the reader what to say;
- avoid metric definitions, report-reading instructions, and generic sports trivia.

They should not:

- explain obvious seasonality;
- repeat the chart in prose;
- tell the reader how to interpret the dashboard;
- provide unsupported sports history, rivalry claims, records, injuries, trades, or rumors;
- imply that the reader lacks business knowledge.

---

## 2. Standing data-scope rule

In this report, **Combined** data covers only the reported business lines:

- Fanatics.com
- wholesale partners
- IVR + events

It does **not** include:

- Fanatics-powered partner sites such as MLB.com, NBA.com, NFL.com, other league stores, or college stores;
- affiliated brands such as Mitchell & Ness, Topps, and Lids, unless those are explicitly included in the source report.

### How to write with this scope

Use light-touch scope language when making broad claims:

- “reported combined demand”
- “for the reported business lines”
- “within the reported channels”
- “in the reported data”
- “directionally, this suggests…”

Do **not** over-caveat every sentence. The reader should feel precision, not legalese.

### Avoid overclaiming

Avoid phrases like:

- “Fanatics demand overall…”
- “the total Fanatics ecosystem…”
- “overall marketplace demand…”
- “all partner-site demand…”

unless the underlying source explicitly includes those channels.

---

## 3. Report-date and source-family rules

Cowork's dashboard date is the **report date**. The underlying component reports usually represent the **prior-day business data**.

Example:

- `report_date: 2026-07-15`
- `data_date: 2026-07-14`

Write commentary against the dashboard section and the represented business data, but be clear when external sports context is tied to the report-date window versus the prior-day data window.

The public events feed uses two source families:

- `espn_rss`: cleaner editorial baseline; stronger default source for factual sports context.
- `yahoo_sports_rss`: broader discovery layer; useful for player/team/fan-culture signals but noisier.

Do not confuse story volume with fact certainty. Yahoo Sports can help surface what fans may be seeing, but any strong historical, rivalry, injury, trade, record, or milestone claim should be verified against official sources, ESPN/AP/Reuters-level journalism, or omitted.

Yahoo News sports is intentionally excluded for now because initial validation showed lower relevance and higher safety/noise risk for this dashboard use case.

## 4. Daily writing loop

Use this loop for each daily report.

### Step 1 — Scope-tag the evidence

Before writing, tag each key observation:

- Fanatics.com only
- wholesale
- IVR/events
- reported combined
- daily forecast
- MTD
- LY comp
- category/league signal
- team/player/product signal
- partner/channel signal
- external sports/culture signal

Ask: **What does this evidence actually cover?**

### Step 2 — Classify the fan/sports moment

For each team, player, league, or product signal, classify the moment:

1. **Active tournament/playoff/championship demand**  
   Tied to a current match, round, semifinal/final window, title chase, or live competitive event.

2. **Recently passed / residual demand**  
   Interest remains after the main sports catalyst has passed.

3. **Evergreen team/city/lifestyle demand**  
   Durable fanbase, city identity, iconic team, logo, or fashion/lifestyle interest.

4. **Player-led / global-star demand**  
   Interest follows the athlete more than the current team or tournament status.

5. **Product/drop/collector demand**  
   Interest is tied to scarcity, collectibles, limited drops, cards, collaborations, or launch timing.

6. **Unclear catalyst**  
   If the catalyst is not verified, do not invent one.

### Step 3 — Identify the commercial signal

Determine what the data is actually showing:

- demand quality
- conversion pressure
- channel mix
- forecast miss or beat
- category softness
- product-level strength
- player/team concentration
- partner-channel divergence
- margin / AOV / AUV mix
- fast-moving event demand

### Step 4 — Map realistic levers

Separate fast levers from slower or conditional ones.

Fast / near-same-day levers may include:

- paid media weighting;
- search bids and keyword emphasis;
- paid social weighting;
- CRM/email/SMS emphasis;
- homepage/category modules;
- merchandising rails;
- onsite search/promoted products;
- remarketing pools;
- retail media weighting;
- partner-channel emphasis where placements are available.

Conditional levers depend on assets, approvals, or placement availability:

- creative swaps using existing assets;
- retail media creative changes;
- player/team-specific messaging;
- event landing pages if templates exist;
- influencer posts if talent/assets/approvals are ready.

Slower levers should not be implied as immediate fixes:

- net-new creative concepts;
- new campaign architecture;
- new photo/video production;
- new brand campaigns;
- major landing-page builds;
- new partnership packages.

A good phrasing pattern:

> Media, search, CRM, and merchandising emphasis can move quickly where assets and placements are available; new creative follows where production and approvals allow.

### Step 5 — Write direct report prose

Use this structure:

1. **Fact** — what happened, scoped correctly.
2. **Interpretation** — what the signal suggests, bounded by source coverage.
3. **Opportunity** — where timing, channel emphasis, or merchandising can matter.

Avoid sounding like the writer is thinking aloud.

## 5. Tone and phrasing rules

### Preferred voice

- senior
- direct
- concise
- commercially literate
- culturally fluent
- fact-grounded
- energized, but not hype-driven

### Avoid telling the reader what to think

Avoid:

> The Fanatics.com read is more constructive than the topline miss alone suggests.

Prefer:

> Yesterday Fanatics.com performance was below forecast; AOV and margin were strong.

Avoid:

> The league contribution is useful as a signal of where demand has reason to concentrate.

Prefer:

> League contribution highlights where demand is concentrating.

Avoid:

> That creates a sharper read.

Prefer stating the read directly.

### Avoid awkward “this more than that” contrasts

Avoid abstract contrast if the sentence is not fully formed:

> The shape of the day points less to a lack of customer interest and more to a need to route existing intent.

Prefer shaping language:

> With customer interest evident, the opportunity is routing that interest toward the moments showing heat.

### Use natural sports/business language

Avoid:

- “live urgency”
- “real global-star signal”
- “likely perishable”
- “does not erase”
- “residual or player-led interest than live tournament momentum”

Prefer:

- “active tournament demand”
- “current tournament heat”
- “semifinal/final window”
- “player-led demand”
- “global-star demand”
- “timing-sensitive”
- “fast-moving demand window”
- “sits alongside”
- “may mask”
- “may divert attention from”
- “residual or player-led interest”

## 6. Fact discipline for sports and culture

Sports color is valuable only when accurate and relevant.

Before adding sports context, verify:

- Is the team/player still active in the tournament/playoffs?
- What round or stage is happening now?
- Has the relevant match/event already happened?
- Is this pre-event, during-event, or post-event demand?
- Is the player actually connected to the team/product mentioned?
- Is the rivalry/history/milestone claim sourced?

Preferred sources:

1. official league, tournament, or team sites;
2. ESPN, AP, Reuters, or comparable reliable sports journalism;
3. official schedules, brackets, standings;
4. official team/player announcements.

Do not add the following unless verified:

- rivalry history;
- “first since” claims;
- title droughts;
- injuries;
- trades;
- retirements;
- record chases;
- fan traditions;
- revenge-game framing;
- “historic” claims.

If a fact is not verified, omit it. A plausible but false sports detail is worse than no detail.

## 7. Section guidance

### Daily Topline

Purpose: explain the top-level demand shape for the reported business lines.

Do:

- scope Combined as reported business lines;
- distinguish Fanatics.com, wholesale, and IVR/events;
- separate day-level forecast performance from MTD and LY context;
- identify whether the issue is conversion, basket quality, channel mix, or demand quality.

Avoid:

- implying Combined equals total Fanatics ecosystem demand;
- using a one-day miss to imply a structural trend;
- saying the reader should be more or less concerned.

### Primary Drivers

Purpose: identify the strongest demand drivers and their current moment status.

Do:

- name the player/team/product signals;
- distinguish active tournament demand from residual or player-led demand;
- connect search/product behavior to commercial interest;
- include only verified sports context.

Avoid:

- treating all player/team demand as equally urgent;
- forcing historical rivalry color;
- over-indexing on eliminated teams as if the competition window is still active.

### League Contribution

Purpose: show where demand is concentrating by league/category, without flattening product-level nuance.

Do:

- use phrase patterns like “League contribution highlights…”;
- identify broad league strength or softness;
- surface product/program pockets under softer aggregates;
- frame NFL/NCAA as forward ramps when appropriate.

Avoid:

- implying a league cannot work because the aggregate is soft;
- suggesting football should be at peak demand before its core window;
- confusing league demand with product/drop demand.

### Combined Performance

Purpose: explain channel mix across reported lines.

Do:

- state which reported business line carried demand;
- identify whether Fanatics.com pressure is conversion, basket, traffic, or forecast;
- use partner/channel signals carefully;
- avoid total-ecosystem claims.

Avoid:

- treating Combined as all Fanatics demand;
- calling partner-site or affiliated-brand trends unless those data are present.

### Growth Drivers

Purpose: identify demand pockets with practical near-term potential.

Do:

- focus on players, teams, countries, products, collaborations, and partner surfaces showing signal;
- separate fast emphasis shifts from slower creative/campaign work;
- use “where assets and placements are available” when relevant.

Avoid:

- assuming current creative already includes hot players or teams;
- implying net-new campaigns can be built immediately.

### Program Outlook

Purpose: connect upcoming programs to the kind of fan demand they can capture.

Do:

- classify programs by demand logic: event, launch, collectible, player, collaboration, campus/lifestyle, seasonal ramp;
- show sequencing across the next week/month;
- connect programs to current demand where supported.

Avoid:

- treating all launches as equal;
- making broad claims about a program without a fan reason to care.

### Demand Trends

Purpose: synthesize the broader pattern from the day.

Do:

- identify whether demand is active, residual, player-led, product/drop-led, or city/lifestyle;
- connect timing-sensitive demand to fast available levers;
- maintain reported-scope discipline.

Avoid:

- using vague phrases like “momentum is building” without evidence;
- overstating trend durability from one day of data.

## 8. Final review checklist

Before finalizing Contextual Notes, run this checklist.

### Scope

- [ ] Does “Combined” mean reported business lines only?
- [ ] Does the note avoid implying total Fanatics ecosystem performance?
- [ ] Are Fanatics.com-only insights kept distinct from reported Combined insights?
- [ ] Are partner-site and affiliated-brand gaps silently respected?

### Time horizon

- [ ] Is the metric daily, MTD, LY, event-window, or forecast-based?
- [ ] Is a one-day miss framed as a one-day miss?
- [ ] Are MTD and LY trends separated from yesterday performance?

### Sports/fan context

- [ ] Is the team/player currently active, recently eliminated, evergreen, or player-led?
- [ ] Is tournament/playoff/championship demand treated as timing-sensitive?
- [ ] Are rivalry/history/milestone claims sourced?
- [ ] Are eliminated teams framed as residual or player-led where appropriate?

### Operational realism

- [ ] Are fast levers separated from creative/campaign-dependent levers?
- [ ] Does the note avoid assuming the right creative assets already exist?
- [ ] Does it mention “where assets and placements are available” when needed?
- [ ] Does the note focus on surfaces that can plausibly move inside the current window?

### Tone

- [ ] Does the note sound direct and senior?
- [ ] Does it avoid telling the reader how to think?
- [ ] Does it avoid metric definitions and generic sports background?
- [ ] Does it retain point of view without overclaiming?

## 9. Example patterns from the 2026-07-12 report

These are examples of the desired approach. Do not copy blindly; update facts and context each day.

### Scope-aware topline

> Reported combined demand finished at $3.03M across the reported business lines, with in-venue carrying the day at $1.64M, or 54% of demand, up +14.1% to LY. Fanatics.com delivered $1.20M and wholesale added $0.20M.

### Day-level performance with time horizon

> Yesterday Fanatics.com performance was below forecast; AOV and margin were strong. Demand missed forecast by 5.2% and CVR was down 34.3%, while AOV rose 13.3%, AUV rose 14.2%, margin expanded 860 bps, and GMM was up 19.8%.

### Active vs residual sports demand

> Argentina and England are both alive heading into the semifinal, giving Messi/Argentina and England/Kane/Bellingham the strongest current tournament context. England eliminated Norway in the quarterfinals, so Norway-related demand should be read as residual or player-led interest. Haaland’s Man City jersey demand keeps him relevant as global-star demand, just not as a current tournament catalyst.

### Product-level strength under aggregate softness

> MLB was the broadest negative mover at -9.5, but that softness may mask product-level strength. MLB ASG Topps NOW, Topps Chrome Baseball, Ohtani Day, KAWS Yankees/Dodgers, Timberland MLB/New Era, and Mitchell & Ness all point to pockets that can work through collectibles, named athletes, collaborations, and drop culture.

### Operationally realistic actionability

> Media, search, CRM/SMS/email weighting, homepage/category modules, retail media, partner-channel emphasis, remarketing, onsite search, and merchandising surfaces can move quickly where assets and placements are available. Creative, influencer, and campaign changes should follow where production, rights, and approvals allow.

## 10. Output expectation

For each dashboard section, produce one concise Contextual Note, usually one or two paragraphs.

Each note should answer:

1. What is the meaningful signal?
2. What external or cultural context changes how to interpret it?
3. What is the practical commercial implication, bounded by the source data?

The final output should be ready to insert into the dashboard with minimal editing.
